In This Text Set

This text set includes three informational texts, a map, and a photo essay. All deal with solving weather-related problems.

Living in Places with Extreme Weather is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign Living in Places with Extreme Weather for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the chart below to see where specific text structures and text features appear across the texts in this set.

Standards and Objectives

  • Science Standard

    Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

  • Informational Text Focus

    In this text set, students will read informational texts as they recognize the ways compare and contrast, cause and effect, and problem and solution text structures help them understand the information they are reading. Students will also reinforce their recognition of the value of text features such as section heads, photographs, captions, and maps to enhance their understanding.

  • Content Objective

    Students will understand types of weather hazards and evaluate ways of solving or preparing for weather-related problems.

  • Language Objective

    Students will describe a variety of weather hazards, as well as related problems and solutions.

How do people solve weather-related issues?

Living in Places with Extreme Weather

by Michael Patiño
Informational Text - Word Count: 523   Lexile: 610L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

  • Content Objective Students will understand strategies for creating homes able to withstand a variety of weather-related challenges.

  • Language Objective Students will explain which types of homes are most effective in withstanding a variety of types of weather.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a problem and solution structure helps explain how to solve a weather-related issue.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how section heads help them recognize the kind of information they are about to read.

Summary

This text explains effective features of homes in places where people face a variety of types of extreme weather.

Before Reading

BIG QUESTION

Remind students of the Big Question: How do people solve weather-related issues? Ask: How do you think Living in Places with Extreme Weather will help you think about the Big Question? What might be some problems in places with extreme weather? How do you think people could solve the problems?

PREVIEW VOCABULARY

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words hazard, hurricane, and climate. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What is an example of a weather hazard? What are some ways people can stay safe in a hurricane? How would you describe the climate where we live?

ORAL VOCABULARY

Some of the vocabulary in Living in Places with Extreme Weather might be new or challenging, such as solution or benefit. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When we have a problem, we look for a solution. One weather problem is that a flood could damage a house. What is a solution to this problem? Or: A benefit is a good thing. How do thick walls benefit people living in places where storms happen?

TWO-COLUMN CHART

Display a two-column chart with the first column labeled Problems and the second column labeled Solutions. Guide students to create similar two-column charts of their own. Encourage students to add weather problems and their solutions to the chart as they read, and include vocabulary words from the text. A completed chart might look like this:

Problems Solutions
hurricane
cold climate		 dome shape
insulation

During Reading

ELEMENTS OF INFORMATIONAL TEXT

Text Structure Explain that Living in Places with Extreme Weather is written with a problem and solution text structure. Say: In each part of the text, the writer describes a problem related to the weather, then a way of solving the problem. As you read, pay attention to the problem and solution in each part.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Problem and Solution Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells how a problem can be solved”

Text Features Preview the text. Point out the heading Wind! Say: Look at this word. It’s bigger and bolder than the rest of the words in the section, because it is the heading. Headings help tell what the section is about. Display screen 8 (Printable Text p. 7). Ask: What is the heading of this section? (Icy Cold Weather!) Ask: What does the heading tell you about the section? What do you think it will be about?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “show what a section will be about”

After Reading

VOCABULARY

After students have read Living in Places with Extreme Weather, have them review their completed two-column charts. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from Living in Places with Extreme Weather to help students as they think about words related to the weather, such as flood, hurricane, and climate.

DISCUSS ACROSS TEXTS

Encourage students to make connections among Living in Places with Extreme Weather and other texts they have read about related subjects.

  • In Living in Places with Extreme Weather, you learned about floods. What other facts have you read about floods?
  • Living in Places with Extreme Weather tells how people live in places with weather problems. What other weather problems have you read about? How do people solve the problems or prepare for them?
  • Think about other texts you’ve read about extreme weather. What more did you learn from reading Living in Places with Extreme Weather?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Solving Weather Problems: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Living in Places with Extreme Weather row with something they learned about solving weather problems.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How do people solve weather-related issues? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as the following: “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What problems can severe weather cause?
  • How can a special way of building a home solve a weather problem?

WRITE ABOUT THE BIG QUESTION

Tell students: Choose one kind of weather hazard from the text. Write a sentence to explain the problem. Then, write about a type of home that helps to solve the problem. Draw the weather problem and the solution.

Raising Chicago

by Ezra Greene
Informational Text - Word Count: 698   Lexile: 690L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

  • Content Objective Students will understand a weather-related problem from history and evaluate solutions to the problem.

  • Language Objective Students will explain the flooding problem in Chicago during the 1800s and the way it was solved.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a problem-and-solution text presents a problem and its solution. They will recognize that some attempted solutions might be unsuccessful.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how section headings give important information about what they are getting ready to read.

Summary

This text explains the need to solve problems with flooding that caused illness and property damage in 1850s Chicago. It explains why one attempted solution did not work and why another was successful.

Before Reading

BIG QUESTION

Remind students of the Big Question: How do people solve weather-related issues? Ask: How do you think Raising Chicago will help you consider the Big Question? Think about the title and how it might relate to a weather-related issue or solution.

PREVIEW VOCABULARY

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words flooding and moisture. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What causes flooding? What kinds of weather might involve moisture?

ORAL VOCABULARY

Some of the vocabulary in Raising Chicago might be new or challenging, such as solution or issue. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: What are some issues or problems that flooding causes? Or: What were some solutions people tried? Why didn't the first solution work?

TWO-COLUMN CHART

Share a two-column chart with the headings Problems and Solutions. Guide students to create two-column charts of their own. Encourage students to add words related to the problems and solutions from the text as they read. For example, they may add the words floods and moisture in the Problems column, and the words planks and raising the streets in the Solutions column.

Problems Solutions
floods
moisture		 planks
raising the streets

During Reading

ELEMENTS OF INFORMATIONAL TEXT

Text Structure Explain that Raising Chicago is written with a problem-and-solution text structure. Say: In this text structure, the writer first gives details about a problem or issue. Then the writer explains how people tried to fix the problem. Tell students that sometimes, a problem-and-solution text will describe more than one solution to a problem. Encourage students to think about which solutions work and don’t work as they read.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Problem-and-Solution Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells how a problem can be solved,” “explains different ways of solving a problem”

Text Features Say: Think about texts you have read that have phrases or sentences in large and bold text. These are called headings. They tell you about what you are about to read. Point out the bold heading A Failed Solution. Ask: What do you think this section will be about? Guide students to understand that the heading tells them they will read about a solution that was tried, but didn’t work.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “tell the topic or main idea of a section”

After Reading

VOCABULARY

After students have read Raising Chicago, have them review their completed two-column charts. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from Raising Chicago to help students as they think about words related to flooding, such as flood, issue, and solution.

DISCUSS ACROSS TEXTS

Encourage students to make connections between Raising Chicago and other texts they have read about related subjects.

  • In Raising Chicago, you learned about a problem with floods. What facts have you read in other texts about floods?
  • Raising Chicago tells a solution for flooding that failed. What else have you read about solutions to weather problems? How well did they work?
  • In Living in Places with Extreme Weather, you read about ways of building homes in places with flooding problems. How does that information relate to what you read in Raising Chicago?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Solving Weather Problems: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Raising Chicago row with something they learned about flooding or solving problems with floods.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How do people solve weather-related issues? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as the following: “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Why did the first solution to the flooding problem fail?
  • Was raising Chicago’s streets and buildings a good solution? Why or why not?
  • What other solutions might work for problems with flooding?

WRITE ABOUT THE BIG QUESTION

Tell students: Suppose that you are a newspaper reporter in Chicago during the 1800s. Write a story about raising the city streets and buildings. Explain what the workers did and why they did it.

Staying Safe from Hurricanes

by Andrea Kennedy
Informational Text - Word Count: 486   Lexile: 810L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

  • Content Objective Students will understand how hurricanes form and learn methods for remaining safe during a hurricane.

  • Language Objective Students will explain ways to stay safe during hurricanes.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a cause-and-effect text explains what happens and why the event(s) happened.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how headings can help them understand the information in a text.

Summary

This text explains the dangers people face from hurricanes and the ways people can stay safe from these dangers.

Before Reading

BIG QUESTION

Remind students of the Big Question: How do people solve weather-related issues? Ask: How do you think Staying Safe from Hurricanes will help you consider the Big Question? What problems do hurricanes cause? How can people solve these problems?

PREVIEW VOCABULARY

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words evacuate, hurricane, and meteorologists. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What kind of weather would mean people might need to evacuate? How would you describe a hurricane? Why do we count on meteorologists to help us?

ORAL VOCABULARY

Some of the vocabulary in Staying Safe from Hurricanes might be new or challenging, such as predict or material. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: What material would protect a house from strong winds? Or: When you predict something, you think that it will happen. How can someone predict a storm?

TWO-COLUMN CHART

Share a two-column chart. To guide critical thinking about weather problems and their solutions, have students create two-column charts of their own, labeling the first column Problems and the second column Solutions. Encourage students to use vocabulary words as they think of problems and solutions to add to their charts—for example, the word hurricanes in the Problems column, with the corresponding word evacuate in the Solutions column.

Problems Solutions
hurricane
floods		 evacuate
build homes from strong materials, create barriers

During Reading

ELEMENTS OF INFORMATIONAL TEXT

Text Structure Explain that most of Staying Safe from Hurricanes has a cause-and-effect text structure. Say: An effect is an event that happens. A cause is why an event happens. Look for clue words that describe causes and effects, such as because and as a result. Direct students to the section "What Are the Effects of a Hurricane?" Guide them to identify clue words for a cause-and-effect structure, such as effects, cause, and lead to.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Cause-and-Effect Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells why events happen”

Text Features Say: Think about texts you have you read that have headings. A heading can help readers find information quickly. It can also show the structure of a text. Ask students to find the first heading in the text. Ask: What does this heading show about the text structure? Guide students to understand that the heading "What Causes a Hurricane to Form" helps to show that the text will be about causes and effects.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write "Headings." Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: "help readers find information quickly," "help show the text structure"

After Reading

VOCABULARY

After students have read Staying Safe from Hurricanes, have them review their completed two-column charts. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from Staying Safe from Hurricanes to help students as they think about words related to severe weather, including evacuate, hurricane, and materials.

DISCUSS ACROSS TEXTS

Encourage students to make connections between Staying Safe from Hurricanes and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Staying Safe from Hurricanes, you learned about the problems created by hurricanes––and the ways to be safe. What facts have you read in other texts about hurricanes or other powerful storms?
  • Staying Safe from Hurricanes explains why a hurricane is dangerous. What other texts have you read that explain why a type of extreme weather is dangerous?
  • Think about information you learned from reading Living in Places with Extreme Weather. As you’ve read Staying Safe from Hurricanes, what other facts have you learned about preparing a home for hurricanes?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Solving Weather Problems: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Staying Safe from Hurricanes row with something they learned about preparing for severe weather.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How do people solve weather-related issues? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as the following: “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Why is it important to prepare for a hurricane?
  • If you were preparing for a hurricane, what would you do first? Why?

WRITE ABOUT THE BIG QUESTION

Tell students: Imagine that you’re a meteorologist, someone who studies the weather and tells people about the kind of weather to expect. Write a short weather report. In your report, explain how you know a hurricane is coming. Tell at least three things people should do to prepare.

Solving Weather Problems



Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read some or all of the texts in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Ask students to review their two-column charts. Then, on a single sheet of paper, have students create two word webs, side-by-side: one labeled Problems in the center and the other labeled Solutions in the center. Tell students to write words or phrases on each web to show weather problems and solutions they read about. Then ask them to draw arrows connecting the problems to the solutions.

Discuss The Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion related to the Big Question: How do people solve weather-related issues? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their graphic organizers as they can.

  • What did you already know about problems with severe weather before you began reading this text set?
  • You’ve read many books about weather problems and solutions. What new information have you learned?
  • What would you still like to learn about preparing for extreme weather or solving weather problems?
  • Tell about some texts you’ve read that are most useful in helping you answer the Big Question.

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: Designing a Home for Extreme Weather

Remind students of types of severe weather they have read about, including hurricanes, tornadoes, floods, and extremely hot or cold weather. Ask them to choose one weather hazard, then use what they learned to design a house or building that helps protect people from the hazard. Ask students to create a diagram with labels to point out features of the building, then write a paragraph explaining how the features keep people safe.

Have students present and discuss their diagrams. Guide the class to discuss each solution and add other ideas for solving the weather problem.

Students can refer to information they have written in Write About the Big Question for each text to aid their thinking and planning. They may also use the Project Template to help them organize and clarify their thinking as they plan their projects. Encourage students to use the vocabulary they’ve learned from the texts in the booklets and presentations.

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project. If they are working with a partner, remind them to work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don't keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
